What?

This week’s challenge was to guide a new intern on the LiveMesh interface team through the world of software architecture. With no prior experience in distributed systems, our task was to create a clear, developer-friendly comparison of four major architectural patterns: monolith, modular monolith, microservices, and serverless. Each needed a definition, pros and cons, a visual diagram, and guidance on when to use each — all from a mentor's perspective.

So What?

This case study reminded me that good developers must also be good teachers. It’s not enough to know which architecture to choose — we must be able to explain why. Each pattern has its place: monoliths offer simplicity, microservices scale independently, modular monoliths balance complexity and structure, and serverless can reduce overhead for event-driven apps. Learning to break down these concepts in an accessible way sharpened both my own understanding and my communication skills.

Now What?

Moving forward, I want to be more intentional about architectural decisions in my own projects, and be ready to explain them clearly to others. Whether I'm mentoring a junior developer or making a technical pitch, I’ll carry forward this approach: define, compare, visualize, and contextualize. I also plan to expand my knowledge of hybrid patterns and architectural trade-offs in real-world deployments — because when systems grow, structure matters.
